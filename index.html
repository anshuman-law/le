What happens when law changes the cost of behaviour?
Core idea

Law is also a behavioural instrument.

In law and economics, we ask not only what a legal rule says, but what it does after people, firms, institutions, and regulators respond to it.

Round 1 · Scarcity and Allocation

The Parking Problem

The college has 100 parking applicants but only 40 parking slots. The administration must choose one rule.

Scarcity means not everyone can get what they want. The legal or administrative rule will decide who receives access, who is excluded, and what behaviour people adopt in response.
Round 2 · Deterrence and Enforcement

The Speeding Problem

A city road has frequent speeding and accidents. The authority wants to reduce speeding without creating unnecessary costs.

Round 3 · Externalities and Liability

The Factory Pollution Problem

A factory produces useful goods and creates jobs, but it pollutes a nearby river. The law must decide who should bear the cost of pollution.

An externality arises when one person or firm’s activity imposes costs on others. If the legal system does nothing, the factory may not fully account for the social cost of pollution.
Final synthesis

What did the Rule Machine show?

Across parking, speeding, and pollution, the central lesson is that legal rules produce behavioural consequences.

1. Law allocates scarcity.

When resources are limited, rules decide access, exclusion, priority, and distribution.

2. Law changes incentives.

People respond to costs, benefits, sanctions, defaults, and enforcement design.

3. Enforcement matters.

A severe penalty may fail if the probability of being caught is too low.

4. Rules create trade-offs.

A rule may improve compliance but increase cost, reduce convenience, or invite evasion.

5. Externalities justify intervention.

Law can shift social costs back to the party that causes them.

6. Good legal design asks consequences.

The question is not only “Is this rule valid?” but also “What will this rule do?”

Final question for class: What makes a legal rule “good” — fairness, efficiency, low cost, high compliance, or some balance among them?
